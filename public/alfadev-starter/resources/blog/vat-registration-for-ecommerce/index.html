VAT Registration for E-commerce: A Complete Guide

10 January 2025 • Page Ivy Team

VAT can be confusing for e-commerce businesses—especially when you’re selling across multiple platforms and potentially internationally. This guide clarifies when you must register, when you might want to, and how different schemes work.

When You MUST Register

The £90,000 Threshold (2025)

You must register for VAT if your taxable turnover exceeds £90,000 in any rolling 12-month period.

Important: This includes sales across all platforms:

  • Amazon UK
  • eBay UK
  • Etsy
  • Your Shopify store
  • Any other UK sales channels

Example: If you make £40k on Amazon, £30k on eBay, and £25k on Shopify, your total is £95k—you must register.

The 30-Day Rule

You must register within 30 days of exceeding the threshold. Register at: gov.uk/register-for-vat

Penalties for Late Registration

  • HMRC can backdate VAT to when you should have registered
  • You’ll owe 20% VAT on all sales during that period
  • You can’t reclaim VAT on purchases from before registration
  • Plus potential penalties and interest

When You Might Want to Voluntarily Register

Even below £90k, voluntary registration can make sense:

1. Reclaim VAT on Purchases

If you’re buying inventory, equipment, and services with VAT, you can reclaim it once registered.

Example:

  • Annual inventory purchases: £30,000 + £6,000 VAT
  • Amazon fees (VAT-exempt): £5,000
  • Marketing spend: £2,000 + £400 VAT
  • Equipment: £3,000 + £600 VAT

VAT you can reclaim: £7,000/year

If your sales are low, you’ll get refunds. If your sales have VAT due, it offsets what you owe.

2. Appear More Professional

Some B2B customers prefer VAT-registered suppliers. It adds credibility.

3. Prepare for Growth

If you’re close to £90k, register early to avoid the last-minute rush and potential compliance issues.

4. Access to Flat Rate Scheme

Once registered, you can use the Flat Rate Scheme, which can reduce your VAT burden (more below).

Standard VAT vs. Flat Rate Scheme

Standard VAT (Default)

  • Charge 20% VAT on all sales
  • Reclaim VAT on purchases
  • File quarterly returns
  • More admin and record-keeping

Best for: Businesses with high VAT-able costs (lots of inventory purchases).

Flat Rate Scheme (Simplified)

  • Charge 20% VAT to customers
  • Pay a fixed percentage to HMRC (depends on industry)
  • E-commerce: 8.5% (or 7.5% in your first year)
  • Keep the difference
  • Can’t reclaim VAT on purchases (except capital assets over £2,000)
  • Less admin

Example:

Sales: £100,000

  • VAT charged to customers: £20,000 (20%)
  • VAT paid to HMRC: £8,500 (8.5% of £100,000)
  • You keep: £11,500

Best for: Businesses with low VAT-able costs (dropshipping, digital products, or high margins).

Which Scheme is Better for E-commerce?

It depends on your gross profit margin and VAT-able costs.

Use Flat Rate if:

  • You’re dropshipping (low inventory costs)
  • High margin products (over 40%)
  • Mostly platform fees (which are VAT-exempt or outside scope)

Use Standard VAT if:

  • You’re buying lots of inventory from UK suppliers
  • Low margins (under 25%)
  • Significant VAT-able expenses

Pro Tip: We can model both scenarios with your real numbers to see which saves more.

VAT on Amazon & Marketplaces

Amazon FBA (UK)

  • All UK sales are subject to VAT once registered
  • Amazon can handle VAT calculation if you enable it
  • Still your responsibility to file returns

Amazon Pan-European FBA

  • Storing stock in EU countries triggers VAT registration requirements in those countries
  • Post-Brexit, you need separate EU VAT registrations
  • Consider using IOSS (Import One-Stop Shop) for low-value goods

eBay

  • Similar to Amazon: you charge VAT on sales, file returns
  • eBay Managed Payments includes VAT in transaction reports

Shopify

  • You configure VAT settings
  • Shopify can calculate VAT automatically
  • You’re responsible for filing returns

International Sales & VAT

Selling to UK Customers

  • Always charge 20% VAT (once registered)

Selling to EU Customers (Post-Brexit)

  • Under €150 value: Use IOSS to collect VAT at point of sale
  • Over €150: Customer pays import VAT on delivery
  • B2B sales: Reverse charge (customer accounts for VAT)

Selling to Rest of World

  • Generally VAT-free
  • Customer may pay import duties/taxes in their country

VAT and Returns

When customers return products:

  1. Issue a credit note to reverse the VAT
  2. Adjust your VAT return accordingly
  3. Don’t forget to account for Amazon’s automatic refunds

Failure to properly account for returns can lead to overpaying VAT.

Making Tax Digital (MTD)

All VAT-registered businesses must use MTD-compatible software:

  • Xero, QuickBooks Online, Sage, etc.
  • Submit returns digitally via API
  • Maintain digital records

We help clients set up MTD-compliant systems from day one.

Common E-commerce VAT Mistakes

1. Not Monitoring the Threshold

Multi-platform sellers often don’t realize they’ve crossed £90k until it’s too late.

Solution: Monthly turnover tracking across all channels.

2. Incorrect Platform Fees Treatment

Amazon fees are generally VAT-exempt or outside the scope of VAT. Don’t charge VAT on them, and you can’t reclaim VAT.

Solution: Categorize platform fees separately in your accounting.

3. Forgetting About Returns

Returns reduce your taxable turnover. If you don’t account for them, you overpay VAT.

Solution: Reconcile platform reports with VAT returns, adjusting for refunds.

4. Mixing EU and UK Stock

If you have stock in EU and UK, different VAT rules apply.

Solution: Track inventory location and apply correct VAT treatment.

5. Charging VAT Before Registration

Only charge VAT once you’re registered with an effective date.

Solution: Configure platform settings on the date HMRC confirms.

VAT Registration Checklist

  • Calculate rolling 12-month turnover across all platforms
  • If over £90k (or approaching), register within 30 days
  • Decide: Standard VAT or Flat Rate Scheme?
  • Set up MTD-compatible accounting software
  • Configure VAT settings on all sales platforms
  • Update pricing (if absorbing VAT or passing to customers)
  • Notify existing customers if B2B
  • File first return (quarterly, due 1 month + 7 days after period end)
