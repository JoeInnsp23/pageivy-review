Self Assessment Deadlines: A Complete Guide

15 December 2024 • Page Ivy Team

Missing a Self Assessment deadline can cost you hundreds—even thousands—in penalties. This guide ensures you never miss another deadline and understand exactly what’s due when.

Key Dates for 2024/25 Tax Year

5 April 2025

End of Tax Year

  • The 2024/25 tax year ends
  • Any income earned up to midnight on 5 April counts for this year
  • Income from 6 April onwards is 2025/26

5 October 2025

Registration Deadline

  • Register for Self Assessment if you’re newly self-employed
  • Or if you have new income requiring a tax return
  • Miss this and you face penalties

31 October 2025

Paper Return Deadline

  • If filing by paper (rare nowadays)
  • HMRC processes and calculates your tax
  • Most people file online instead

31 January 2026

Online Return Deadline (The Big One)

  • File your 2024/25 Self Assessment return online
  • Pay any tax owed for 2024/25
  • Pay first “payment on account” for 2025/26 (if applicable)

This is the deadline most people miss.

31 July 2026

Second Payment on Account

  • Second installment for 2025/26 estimated tax
  • Only if you have payments on account

What is “Payment on Account”?

If your tax bill was over £1,000 last year, HMRC assumes you’ll owe similar this year.

How it works:

Last year’s tax bill: £10,000

  • 31 January 2026: Pay £10,000 (for 2024/25) + £5,000 (first payment on account for 2025/26)
  • 31 July 2026: Pay £5,000 (second payment on account for 2025/26)
  • 31 January 2027: Pay balance (if your actual 2025/26 tax is more than £10k)

Important: You’re paying 1.5 years of tax on 31 January—last year’s final bill plus half of this year’s estimate.

Reducing Payments on Account

If your income has dropped, you can reduce your payments on account:

  • Log into your HMRC account
  • Request reduction
  • Provide reason (e.g., “Income reduced due to business downturn”)

Caution: If you reduce too much, you’ll owe interest on the shortfall.

Penalties for Late Filing

HMRC doesn’t mess around with late returns. Penalties escalate quickly:

1 Day Late

£100 fixed penalty

Even if you owe no tax. Even if you’re only 1 day late.

3 Months Late

£10/day for next 90 days (up to £900)

Plus the initial £100.

Total so far: £1,000

6 Months Late

£300 or 5% of tax owed (whichever is higher)

Plus the previous £1,000.

12 Months Late

Another £300 or 5% of tax owed (whichever is higher)

Plus all previous penalties.

Example:

Tax owed: £5,000 Return filed: 13 months late

  • Initial penalty: £100
  • Daily penalties (90 days): £900
  • 6-month penalty: 5% of £5,000 = £250 (but minimum £300, so £300)
  • 12-month penalty: £300

Total Penalties: £1,600

Plus interest on the unpaid £5,000.

Penalties for Late Payment

Separate from late filing, there are late payment penalties:

30 Days Late

5% of tax owed

6 Months Late

Another 5% of tax owed

12 Months Late

Another 5% of tax owed

Plus Daily Interest

Currently around 7.75% per year on the outstanding balance.

Example:

Tax owed: £10,000 Payment: 13 months late

  • 30-day penalty: £500 (5%)
  • 6-month penalty: £500 (5%)
  • 12-month penalty: £500 (5%)
  • Interest (~7.75% for 13 months): £837

Total Cost: £2,337

Plus the original £10,000 you owed.

Who Needs to File Self Assessment?

You must file if you:

Are Self-Employed

  • Sole trader
  • Partner in a partnership
  • Earning over £1,000 from self-employment

Have Other Income

  • Rental income over £1,000
  • Investment income over £10,000
  • Foreign income
  • Capital gains over the annual exemption (£3,000 in 2024/25)

Are a Company Director

  • Even if you only take a small salary
  • Unless it’s all taxed via PAYE and under £100k

Have Untaxed Income

  • Side hustle income
  • Freelance work
  • Tips and gratuities

High Earners

  • Over £100,000 (to claim back personal allowance)
  • Child Benefit and earn over £50,000

Want to Claim Reliefs

  • Marriage allowance
  • Higher rate pension tax relief

Not sure if you need to file? Check with HMRC or an accountant.

How to File on Time: A Simple System

April-May: Gather Records

  • Invoices and sales records
  • Bank statements
  • Receipts for expenses
  • Platform income reports (YouTube, Amazon, etc.)
  • P60 (if employed as well)

June-August: Organize

  • Categorize expenses
  • Calculate total income by source
  • Identify any unusual items

September-October: Prepare

  • Complete draft return
  • Calculate estimated tax due
  • Set aside money for payment

November-December: Review

  • Double-check figures
  • Ensure all income included
  • Verify expense claims are legitimate

Early January: Submit

  • File online
  • Pay tax owed
  • Aim for mid-January to avoid last-minute rush

Never wait until 31 January. HMRC’s website often crashes on deadline day.

What You Need to File

Income Information

  • Self-employment income (invoices, sales reports)
  • Employment income (P60)
  • Rental income (property records)
  • Investment income (dividend statements)
  • Other income (freelance platforms, crypto, etc.)

Expense Information

  • Receipts for allowable expenses
  • Mileage records
  • Home office calculation
  • Inventory purchases (if applicable)

Personal Information

  • National Insurance number
  • UTR (Unique Taxpayer Reference)
  • Bank details for refunds or payments

Filing Online: Step-by-Step

  1. Create Government Gateway Account (if you don’t have one)

    • Go to: gov.uk/log-in-file-self-assessment-tax-return
    • You’ll need your UTR

  2. Activate Self Assessment

    • HMRC sends activation code by post (7-10 days)
    • Can’t file until you receive it

  3. Complete Return

    • Enter income by source
    • Enter allowable expenses
    • System calculates tax owed

  4. Review

    • Double-check all figures
    • Ensure National Insurance is calculated
    • Review tax calculation

  5. Submit

    • Get submission receipt
    • Save/print for records

  6. Pay Tax Owed

    • By bank transfer, debit card, or direct debit
    • Must clear by 31 January

Payment Options

Bank Transfer

  • Use HMRC bank details
  • Reference: Your UTR + ‘K’ (for Self Assessment)
  • Allow 3-5 working days

Debit Card

  • Online via HMRC website
  • Instant
  • No fees

Direct Debit

  • Set up online
  • Must be at least 5 working days before deadline
  • Automatic payment

Credit Card

  • Not accepted by HMRC directly
  • Can use third-party providers (they charge fees)

What if You Can’t Pay?

If you can’t pay your tax bill on time:

1. Don’t Ignore It

Late payment penalties and interest add up fast.

2. Contact HMRC

  • Call: 0300 200 3822
  • Request a “Time to Pay” arrangement
  • Explain why you can’t pay

3. Set Up Payment Plan

  • HMRC may allow monthly payments
  • Usually up to 12 months
  • Interest still charged (but no penalties if you stick to plan)

4. Pay What You Can

  • Even a partial payment reduces interest and penalties
  • Shows good faith

Common Mistakes

1. Missing the Deadline

Cost: £100 minimum, potentially much more.

Solution: File early. Never leave it to the last minute.

2. Not Saving for Tax

Cost: Cash flow crisis, potential payment plan interest.

Solution: Set aside 25-30% of income throughout the year.

3. Forgetting Income Sources

Cost: HMRC penalties for inaccurate returns.

Solution: Use accounting software to track all income automatically.

4. Over-Claiming Expenses

Cost: HMRC enquiries, potential penalties, stress.

Solution: Only claim genuine, allowable business expenses.

5. Not Keeping Records

Cost: Can’t prove expenses if HMRC asks.

Solution: Digital receipt capture (e.g., Dext, Receipt Bank).
