Managing International Payments: Tax and Accounting Guide

Written by, Page Ivy Team on February 5, 2025

taxinternationalpayments

In today’s digital economy, earning income from multiple countries is the norm rather than the exception. But international payments come with unique accounting and tax challenges that can trip up even experienced business owners. This guide breaks down everything you need to know.

Common International Payment Scenarios

1. Platform Payments (USD/EUR)

  • YouTube AdSense (paid in USD or local currency)
  • Amazon affiliates
  • Patreon
  • Shopify sales in foreign currencies

2. Direct Client Payments

  • Invoicing international clients
  • Receiving wire transfers
  • PayPal/Wise payments from overseas

3. Marketplace Sales

  • Etsy (sales to US customers)
  • Amazon international marketplaces
  • eBay global shipping

Currency Exchange: The Basics

When you receive payment in a foreign currency, you must convert it to GBP for your UK accounts and tax return.

HMRC’s Rules

You can use:

  1. Actual rate on the day - The rate you actually received
  2. HMRC’s monthly average - Published rates (for convenience)
  3. Period average - Your own consistent method

Important: Once you choose a method, stick with it consistently.

Exchange Rate Example

You’re paid $1,000 from YouTube:

  • Actual rate when received: 1.27 (£787.40)
  • HMRC monthly average: 1.25 (£800.00)
  • Either is acceptable, but be consistent

Payment Processors and Fees

PayPal

  • Receives: $1,000
  • Exchange rate: Often 3-4% worse than mid-market
  • Fee: 3.4% + £0.20
  • You receive: ~£733

Tax treatment:

  • Revenue: £733 (what you actually got)
  • Fees: Deductible business expense
  • Exchange rate loss: Built into the revenue figure

Wise (TransferWise)

  • Receives: $1,000
  • Exchange rate: Near mid-market
  • Fee: 0.5-1%
  • You receive: ~£784

Much better for regular international payments

Stripe

  • Similar to PayPal but slightly better rates
  • Can receive and hold multiple currencies
  • Convert when rates are favorable

Accounting for Foreign Currency

Method 1: Spot Rate (Recommended for Most)

Record income at the exchange rate on the day you received it.

Example:

  • Jan 5: Received $500 @ 1.30 = £385
  • Jan 20: Received $500 @ 1.25 = £400
  • Total income: £785

Advantages:

  • Accurate
  • Matches actual cash received
  • Simple to explain

Method 2: Period Average

Use monthly average rates for all transactions that month.

Example:

  • January average: 1.27
  • All January USD income converted @ 1.27
  • Simpler if you have many transactions

Advantages:

  • Less record keeping
  • HMRC approved
  • Good for high transaction volumes

Method 3: Actual Bank Rate

Use the rate your bank/processor actually applied.

Example:

  • PayPal shows £385 for $500 payment
  • Record £385 regardless of market rate

Advantages:

  • Matches bank statements perfectly
  • No separate exchange rate records needed
  • No unexplained differences

Tax Implications

Income Recognition

Foreign income is taxable in the UK if you’re UK resident, regardless of where it’s earned or held.

Key points:

  • Declare all foreign income
  • Convert to GBP for tax return
  • Rates don’t have to be perfect, but must be reasonable
  • Include even if not remitted to UK

Exchange Rate Gains/Losses

If you hold foreign currency accounts:

Trading gain/loss: Part of profit calculation

  • Received $10,000 @ 1.30 = £7,692
  • Later converted @ 1.25 = £8,000
  • Gain: £308 (taxable)

Capital gain/loss: Only for sums over £500

  • Generally doesn’t apply to business accounts
  • Relevant for savings/investments

VAT on International Sales

B2C (Consumers):

  • Goods under £135 to EU: Use One Stop Shop (OSS)
  • Goods over £135 to EU: Customer pays import VAT
  • Services to non-UK consumers: Usually customer’s VAT applies
  • Digital services: Special rules apply

B2B (Businesses):

  • Usually reverse charge applies
  • Customer accounts for VAT in their country
  • You need their VAT number
  • Zero-rated on your invoice

Best Practices for Different Business Types

Content Creators

Platforms: YouTube, Patreon, Substack Typical: USD payments, monthly frequency

Best approach:

  1. Use Wise for receiving platform payments
  2. Convert immediately to GBP (avoid holding USD)
  3. Record actual amount received in GBP
  4. Track payment processor fees separately

E-commerce Sellers

Platforms: Shopify, Amazon, Etsy Typical: Multiple currencies, high volume

Best approach:

  1. Use platform’s currency conversion where available
  2. Reconcile in GBP from bank statements
  3. Use period average method to simplify
  4. Software like A2X for marketplace accounting

Freelancers & Agencies

Payment: Direct invoicing Typical: EUR/USD, variable amounts

Best approach:

  1. Invoice in GBP where possible
  2. If invoicing in foreign currency, set clear payment terms
  3. Use Wise Business for receiving
  4. Bank rate method for simple accounting

Choosing a Payment Processor

Wise (Best for Most)

  • Pros: Great rates, multi-currency accounts, business accounts
  • Cons: Not everyone accepts it
  • Best for: Receiving international payments regularly

PayPal

  • Pros: Widely accepted, instant transfers
  • Cons: Poor exchange rates, high fees
  • Best for: One-off payments, invoicing

Payoneer

  • Pros: Popular with marketplaces, good for USD
  • Cons: Higher fees than Wise
  • Best for: Amazon sellers, marketplaces

Stripe

  • Pros: Multi-currency, good for businesses
  • Cons: Requires website integration
  • Best for: E-commerce, SaaS businesses

Revolut Business

  • Pros: Hold 30+ currencies, good rates
  • Cons: Limited business features vs Wise
  • Best for: Frequent currency exchange, holding multiple currencies

Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. Not Declaring Foreign Income

HMRC can see payments from platforms. Always declare.

2. Using Consumer PayPal for Business

  • Terrible rates
  • No business expense tracking
  • Limited currency options

3. Inconsistent Exchange Rates

Pick a method and stick to it. Don’t cherry-pick favorable rates.

4. Ignoring Small Payments

All income must be declared, even £10 from a Patreon supporter.

5. Not Understanding VAT Rules

Digital services to EU consumers have special rules. Ignore them at your peril.

6. Holding Large USD Balances

Unless you have a specific reason, convert to GBP regularly to avoid exchange rate risk.

7. Not Keeping Exchange Rate Records

If you use spot rates, document them. Save confirmation emails.

Software Solutions

Accounting Software with Multi-Currency

Xero (Recommended)

  • Bank feeds in foreign currencies
  • Automatic conversion
  • Foreign currency invoicing

QuickBooks

  • Multi-currency support

  • US-focused features

FreeAgent

  • Multi-currency invoicing
  • Good for UK freelancers
  • Simpler than Xero

Specialist Tools

A2X - For Amazon/Shopify sellers

  • Reconciles marketplace payments
  • Handles currency conversion
  • Links to Xero/QuickBooks

Dext (Receipt Bank) - For expense tracking

  • Multi-currency receipts
  • Automatic conversion
  • Integrates with accounting software

Record Keeping Requirements

You must keep:

  1. Payment notifications - Platform emails/screenshots
  2. Bank statements - Showing actual receipts
  3. Exchange rate records - If using spot rates
  4. Fee documentation - PayPal/Wise statements
  5. Invoices - If invoicing in foreign currency

Keep for 6 years from the end of the tax year.
