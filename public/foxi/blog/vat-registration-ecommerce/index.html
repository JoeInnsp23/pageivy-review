VAT Registration for E-commerce: When and Why?

Written by, Page Ivy Team on January 22, 2025

vatecommercecompliance

VAT registration is one of the biggest milestones for growing e-commerce businesses. But it’s also one of the most confusing aspects of UK tax. This guide cuts through the jargon to help you understand when to register, which scheme to choose, and how to manage VAT across multiple selling channels.

When Must You Register for VAT?

You must register for VAT if your taxable turnover exceeds £90,000 in any rolling 12-month period (2024/25 threshold).

What Counts as Taxable Turnover?

  • All UK sales (excluding exempt items)
  • Sales to EU consumers above the distance selling threshold
  • UK services provided
  • Exports (zero-rated but still taxable)

What Doesn’t Count?

  • VAT-exempt sales (insurance, education, some healthcare)
  • Sales outside the scope of UK VAT

The Registration Deadline

You must register within 30 days of breaching the threshold. HMRC backdates your registration to the date you exceeded £90,000, so you’ll owe VAT from that point.

Should You Register Voluntarily?

Even if you’re under the threshold, voluntary registration might make sense:

Reasons to Register Early

1. Claim VAT back on purchases If you’re buying stock, equipment, or services from VAT-registered suppliers, you’re paying 20% VAT. Registration lets you reclaim this.

Example: You spend £10,000 on stock. That includes £1,667 VAT. Register for VAT and reclaim it.

2. Professional credibility B2B buyers often prefer VAT-registered suppliers as they can reclaim the VAT.

3. Future-proofing Getting systems in place before you’re forced to avoids a last-minute panic.

Reasons NOT to Register

1. Primarily B2C sales Your customers can’t reclaim VAT, so you’re adding 20% to your prices (or absorbing it in your margins).

2. Low input VAT If you’re dropshipping or have minimal VAT-able purchases, there’s little to reclaim.

3. Administrative burden VAT returns are quarterly and require detailed records. There’s a compliance cost.

Choosing Your VAT Scheme

The UK offers several VAT schemes for small businesses. Choose wisely - you’re locked in for at least one year.

1. Standard VAT Accounting

How it works:

  • Charge 20% VAT on sales
  • Reclaim VAT on purchases
  • Pay HMRC the difference quarterly

Best for:

  • Businesses with high input VAT (lots of VAT-able purchases)
  • B2B sellers where customers reclaim VAT anyway
  • Businesses with thin margins

Example:

  • Sales: £50,000 + £10,000 VAT = £60,000
  • Purchases: £35,000 + £7,000 VAT = £42,000
  • Pay HMRC: £10,000 - £7,000 = £3,000

2. Flat Rate Scheme (FRS)

How it works:

  • Charge 20% VAT on sales (same as standard)
  • Pay HMRC a fixed percentage of gross turnover (inc. VAT)
  • Can’t reclaim VAT on purchases (except capital assets over £2,000)

Best for:

  • Service businesses with low costs
  • Businesses with minimal VAT-able purchases
  • Simplified accounting preference

Rates by sector:

  • Retail: 7.5%
  • Advertising: 11%
  • Wholesale: 6.5%
  • Catering (takeaway): 12.5%

Example (7.5% rate):

  • Sales including VAT: £60,000
  • Pay HMRC: £60,000 × 7.5% = £4,500
  • Compared to standard: £3,000
  • You keep: £10,000 VAT charged - £4,500 paid = £5,500 extra

First year rate: Reduce the rate by 1% in your first year if you’re newly VAT registered.

3. Cash Accounting Scheme

How it works:

  • Pay VAT when you receive payment (not when you invoice)
  • Reclaim VAT when you pay suppliers (not when you receive invoices)

Best for:

  • Businesses with extended payment terms
  • Improved cash flow management
  • Turnover under £1.35m

4. Annual Accounting Scheme

How it works:

  • Submit one VAT return per year (instead of quarterly)
  • Make 9 monthly payments based on last year’s VAT
  • Reconcile at year-end

Best for:

  • Predictable businesses
  • Those who want fewer returns
  • Turnover under £1.35m

E-commerce Specific VAT Issues

1. Amazon FBA VAT

Amazon acts as a marketplace facilitator for EU sales but not UK sales. You must:

  • Charge UK VAT on UK sales
  • Handle your own UK VAT returns
  • Consider VAT on goods stored in Amazon warehouses

2. eBay VAT

eBay doesn’t handle VAT for you. Track:

  • UK sales (20% VAT)
  • Overseas sales (usually zero-rated)
  • Fees (inc. VAT) for reclaiming

3. Shopify VAT

Configure Shopify to:

  • Collect UK VAT automatically
  • Handle EU VAT if using OSS scheme
  • Separate B2B (VAT exempt with valid number) from B2C sales

4. International Sales

EU Sales:

  • Goods under £135 to consumers: Use One Stop Shop (OSS)
  • Goods over £135: Customer pays import VAT
  • Services: Usually customer’s country VAT applies

Non-EU Exports:

  • Zero-rated for VAT
  • Requires export evidence (shipping documents)
  • Must prove goods left the UK

Making Tax Digital (MTD) for VAT

As of April 2022, all VAT-registered businesses must use MTD-compliant software for VAT returns.

What You Need:

  • MTD-compliant accounting software (Xero, QuickBooks, Sage)
  • Digital record-keeping (no Excel spreadsheets allowed)
  • Digital submission of returns

What It Means:

  • Your accounting software must connect directly to HMRC
  • No more manual VAT return entry on HMRC website
  • Automatic data transfer from software to HMRC

Common VAT Mistakes E-commerce Businesses Make

1. Missing the Registration Deadline

HMRC penalties start from day 1 you’re late. Plus, you’ll owe backdated VAT.

2. Using Gross Instead of Net Figures

When checking if you’ve hit the threshold, use sales excluding VAT (net), not including VAT (gross).

3. Not Claiming VAT on Startup Costs

You can reclaim VAT on pre-registration expenses up to 4 years before registration (goods) or 6 months (services).

4. Wrong Scheme for Your Business

FRS sounds attractive but might cost you money if you have high input VAT. Model both scenarios before choosing.

5. Forgetting About Partial Exemption

If you sell a mix of VATable and exempt goods, VAT reclaims get complicated. Get professional advice.

VAT Record Keeping Requirements

You must keep:

  • Sales invoices (for 6 years)
  • Purchase invoices
  • Credit notes
  • VAT account showing VAT charged and reclaimed
  • Bank statements
  • Import/export documents

Digital records are required under MTD.
