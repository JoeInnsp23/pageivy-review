Tax Deductions Every Content Creator Should Know

Written by, Page Ivy Team on January 15, 2025

taxcontent-creators

As a content creator, you’re entitled to claim business expenses that reduce your taxable profit. But many creators miss out on legitimate deductions simply because they don’t know what’s allowable. Here’s your comprehensive guide to tax deductions for content creators.

Equipment and Technology

The tools of your trade are fully deductible business expenses:

  • Cameras and photography equipment - Whether it’s a £3000 DSLR or a smartphone upgrade specifically for content creation
  • Computers and laptops - Desktop PCs, MacBooks, tablets used for editing and content management
  • Audio equipment - Microphones, audio interfaces, headphones, and acoustic treatment
  • Lighting equipment - Ring lights, softboxes, LED panels, and light stands
  • Props and backdrops - Anything used specifically for content creation

Capital Allowances vs. Expenses

For equipment over £1000, you can claim the Annual Investment Allowance (AIA), which allows you to deduct the full cost in the first year. For smaller items, they can be expensed immediately.

Software and Subscriptions

All the digital tools that power your content business are deductible:

  • Video editing software (Adobe Creative Cloud, Final Cut Pro, DaVinci Resolve licenses)
  • Project management tools (Notion, Asana, Trello)
  • Stock footage and music licensing
  • Website hosting and domain names
  • Email marketing platforms
  • Analytics and SEO tools

Pro tip: Annual subscriptions are often cheaper than monthly, and you can claim the full year’s cost in your accounts.

Home Office Expenses

If you create content from home, you can claim a proportion of your household costs:

Simplified Method

HMRC allows £6/week (£312/year) with no questions asked - perfect for creators who occasionally use their bedroom as a studio.

Actual Costs Method

For dedicated home studios, calculate the proportion of your home used for business:

  • Rent or mortgage interest
  • Council tax
  • Utilities (electricity, gas, water)
  • Internet and phone
  • Home insurance
  • Repairs and maintenance

Example: If your studio is 100 sq ft in a 1000 sq ft home, you can claim 10% of household costs.

Travel and Transport

Travel for content creation purposes is deductible:

  • Mileage - 45p per mile for the first 10,000 business miles, 25p thereafter
  • Public transport - Trains, tubes, buses for filming locations or events
  • Parking and tolls - Related to business travel
  • Hotel accommodation - When traveling for content creation

Important: Commuting to a regular workplace isn’t deductible, but traveling to filming locations, interviews, or events is.

Content Creation Costs

Expenses directly related to making content:

  • Location fees and permits
  • Talent and appearance fees
  • Props, costumes, and wardrobe
  • Makeup and hair styling (for professional shoots)
  • Set design and construction
  • Catering for crew and talent

Professional Services

  • Accountant fees - Yes, you can deduct the cost of your accountant!
  • Editor and virtual assistant costs
  • Graphic designer fees
  • Legal and professional advice
  • Website development
  • Branding and marketing consultancy

Courses and Training

Education that improves your content creation skills:

  • Online courses (Skillshare, Masterclass, industry-specific training)
  • Conferences and industry events
  • Books and educational materials
  • Workshop fees

Note: Must be relevant to your current business, not training for a completely new venture.

What You CAN’T Claim

Be aware of expenses that aren’t allowable:

  • Personal clothing (unless it’s a costume/uniform)
  • Gym memberships (even if you’re a fitness creator - only deductible for fitness businesses)
  • Food and drink (except when entertaining clients or filming)
  • Fines and penalties
  • Personal grooming (haircuts, nails, general makeup)

Record Keeping Best Practices

To claim these deductions, you need good records:

  1. Keep all receipts - Use apps like Dext or Receipt Bank
  2. Separate bank accounts - Business expenses through business account
  3. Document business purpose - Note what the expense was for
  4. Track mileage - Use apps like MileIQ
  5. Photograph large equipment - Proves business use

The Golden Rule

To be deductible, an expense must be:

  • Incurred wholly and exclusively for business purposes
  • Necessary for your trade
  • Not capital in nature (unless claiming capital allowances)

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Mixing personal and business - That new iPhone is only deductible if used exclusively for business
  2. Claiming too much home use - Be realistic about space and time used for business
  3. No receipts - “I definitely bought it” won’t fly with HMRC
  4. Claiming personal travel as business - Holiday vlogs still count as holidays
