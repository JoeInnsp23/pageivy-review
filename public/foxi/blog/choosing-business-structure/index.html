Sole Trader vs Limited Company: Which Structure Is Right for You?

Written by, Page Ivy Team on January 29, 2025

taxbusiness-structure

One of the most important decisions you’ll make as a new business owner is choosing your legal structure. For most digital entrepreneurs, the choice comes down to sole trader or limited company. Each has distinct advantages and disadvantages - and the right answer depends on your specific situation.

Sole Trader: The Basics

As a sole trader, you and your business are legally one entity. You’re self-employed, and all profits belong to you personally.

Advantages

1. Simple to set up

  • Register with HMRC as self-employed (free, takes 10 minutes online)
  • No Companies House registration
  • Start trading immediately

2. Lower admin burden

  • One tax return per year (self-assessment)
  • Simpler record keeping
  • No annual accounts to file

3. Complete control

  • All decisions are yours
  • No shareholders or directors to consult
  • Easy to wind down if needed

4. Lower costs

  • No company formation fees
  • Typically lower accountancy fees (£300-800/year vs £800-2000/year)
  • No Companies House filing fees

Disadvantages

1. Unlimited liability

  • Your personal assets are at risk if business fails
  • No separation between business and personal debts

2. Higher tax at upper income levels

  • Income tax rates up to 45%
  • Class 2 and Class 4 National Insurance
  • No tax-efficient profit extraction options

3. Less professional perception

  • Some clients prefer working with limited companies
  • Can be harder to win large contracts

4. Harder to raise investment

  • Can’t sell shares
  • Limited access to business financing

Limited Company: The Basics

A limited company is a separate legal entity. You own shares in the company and typically act as a director. The company’s finances are completely separate from your personal finances.

Advantages

1. Tax efficiency (for higher earners)

  • Corporation tax at 19-25% (vs income tax up to 45%)
  • Extract profits as dividends (lower tax rates)
  • More flexibility in timing income

2. Limited liability protection

  • Company debts are separate from personal debts
  • Your assets are protected (within reason)

3. Professional image

  • Perceived as more established
  • Easier to win corporate clients
  • Can help with credibility

4. Easier to raise finance

  • Can sell shares to investors
  • Better borrowing options
  • Can bring on partners/shareholders

Disadvantages

1. Higher admin requirements

  • Annual accounts and Corporation Tax return
  • Confirmation statement to Companies House
  • More complex bookkeeping
  • Statutory records and board minutes

2. Higher costs

  • Company formation (£12-50)
  • Annual accountancy fees (£800-2,000+)
  • Companies House filing fees

3. Less flexibility

  • Can’t just take money out - must follow rules
  • Formal dividend procedures
  • More regulated

4. Public information

  • Directors’ details are public
  • Accounts are public
  • Home address may be visible (unless using service address)

The Tax Comparison

This is where things get interesting. Let’s compare real numbers.

Example: £30,000 Profit

Sole Trader:

  • Income: £30,000
  • Less: Personal Allowance: £12,570
  • Taxable: £17,430
  • Income Tax (20%): £3,486
  • NI Class 4 (9%): £1,356
  • NI Class 2: £177
  • Total Tax: £5,019
  • Take Home: £24,981

Limited Company:

  • Profit: £30,000
  • Corporation Tax (19%): £5,700 (for 2024/25)
  • Remaining: £24,300
  • Salary (below NI threshold): £9,100
  • Dividends: £15,200
  • Tax on dividends*: £0 (covered by dividend allowance)
  • Total Tax: £5,700
  • Take Home: £24,300

*Assuming no other income

At £30,000, there’s very little difference. But as income increases…

Example: £60,000 Profit

Sole Trader:

  • Total Tax & NI: £15,419
  • Take Home: £44,581

Limited Company:

  • Corporation Tax: £9,690
  • Salary + Dividends optimally structured
  • Personal tax on dividends: ~£1,350
  • Total Tax: £11,040
  • Take Home: £48,960

Saving: £4,379/year

Example: £100,000 Profit

Sole Trader:

  • Total Tax & NI: £32,719
  • Take Home: £67,281

Limited Company:

  • Corporation Tax: £17,350
  • Salary + Dividends optimally structured
  • Personal tax on dividends: ~£7,350
  • Total Tax: £24,700
  • Take Home: £75,300

Saving: £8,019/year

The Crossover Point

Generally, limited company becomes more tax-efficient when profits consistently exceed £40,000-50,000 per year.

But tax isn’t everything. Consider:

  • Your risk tolerance
  • Administrative preferences
  • Growth plans
  • Need for professional image
  • Personal circumstances

Special Considerations for Digital Businesses

Content Creators

  • Early stage: Sole trader makes sense
  • Established: Limited company when income exceeds £40-50k
  • Watch out for: Personal Services Companies (IR35) rules

E-commerce

  • Product-based: Consider limited liability early due to product risk
  • Dropshipping: Sole trader often sufficient initially
  • Watch out for: Stock ownership liability

Freelancers & Contractors

  • Low income: Sole trader is simpler
  • IR35 caught: Limited company offers no tax advantage
  • IR35 outside: Limited company highly beneficial over £50k

Can You Switch?

Yes! Many businesses start as sole traders and incorporate later.

Incorporating Your Sole Trade

Process:

  1. Form limited company
  2. Transfer business assets to company
  3. Close sole trader registration
  4. Inform clients and update contracts

Tax considerations:

  • Business assets may trigger capital gains
  • Stock transfers need careful valuation
  • Professional advice essential

Timing:

  • Best done at year-end for cleaner accounts
  • Consider tax year timing
  • Plan for transition period

What About Partnerships and LLPs?

If you’re in business with others:

Partnership:

  • Like sole trader but shared between partners
  • Each partner does self-assessment
  • Unlimited liability (shared)

LLP (Limited Liability Partnership):

  • Limited liability like a company
  • Taxed like a partnership
  • Popular with professional services (accountants, solicitors)
  • Higher admin than partnership, lower than limited company

Making the Decision

Choose Sole Trader if:

  • Profits under £40,000
  • Simplicity is priority
  • Low risk business
  • Just starting out
  • Want minimal admin

Choose Limited Company if:

  • Profits over £50,000
  • Want limited liability
  • Planning significant growth
  • Need professional image
  • Want tax efficiency

In the £40-50k zone: It depends on your personal priorities.

Getting Professional Advice

The right structure depends on:

  • Your profit level
  • Personal tax situation
  • Growth plans
  • Risk profile
  • Administrative capacity
  • IR35 status (if contractor)
  • Other income sources
